## Rationale

### Why a specification entry is required

Recommendations 8 and 28 leave screening intervals as explicit ranges — "every 5 to 10 years" for the general population and "every 3 to 5 years" for WLHIV. The textualist DMN preserves these ranges literally in the `ScreeningInterval` output of the Eligible For Screening decision (`DecisionRule_1yry35i` for general population, `DecisionRule_1925hkc` for WLHIV). The textualist is correct as a faithful reading of the 2021 guideline but is not operationally deployable: a downstream scheduling system or a patient-facing reminder workflow cannot act on a string like "every 5 to 10 years." Operational use requires a specific value.

This is the prototypical `specification` entry per the register schema: the guideline deliberately leaves a range, and the purposive L2 must pick a specific value within that range, citing the underlying evidence base and the scope's epidemiological regime.

### The regime declared in cc-000 is the preconditioning

[`cc-000-scope-declaration.md`](cc-000-scope-declaration.md) declares the epidemiological regime this case study assumes. The regime-relevant elements for the interval specification are:

- **Baseline cervical cancer incidence: moderate to high** (comparable to the East and Southern African context described by Barango in the April 2026 simulated review).
- **HIV prevalence in the screening-age population: moderate to high.** WLHIV are a meaningful fraction of women presenting for screening.
- **HPV vaccination coverage in the aging cohort: low to moderate.** The unvaccinated-cohort assumption inherited from the 2021 guideline is still approximately valid.
- **Existing screening coverage and first-time-screen backlog: low to moderate.** Most women of screening age have not yet been screened. **The binding resource constraint on the screening program is reaching first-time-screenees rather than rescreening previously-negative ones.**

cc-000 explicitly flags the implication for cc-005:

> "Under a fixed screening budget, shortening the rescreen interval competes with first-time-screen coverage, and in a low-coverage regime the evidence-informed policy choice may well be the longer bound of the guideline's range rather than the shorter."

This entry accepts that framing and resolves the intervals accordingly.

### General population: resolved to every 10 years

The resolution for the general population is the upper bound of Rec 8's range: **every 10 years**. Three considerations support this.

**First, the resource-constraint argument from cc-000.** The declared regime has low-to-moderate existing screening coverage and a large first-time-screenee backlog. Under a fixed screening budget, every additional rescreen at year 5 is an opportunity cost against reaching an unscreened woman for the first time. In the regime the case study assumes, the marginal public-health benefit of a first screen for a never-screened woman is substantially larger than the marginal benefit of a second screen at year 5 (versus year 10) for a previously-negative woman. The 10-year interval frees capacity for first-time screenees.

**Second, the evidence from the HPV FOCAL and POBASCAM trials cited in IARC Handbook Vol. 18.** These trials established that HPV-based primary screening's protective duration is longer than one 5-year interval. The lead-time gain from HPV screening (compared to cytology) extends beyond the typical 5-year rescreen window, which is the evidential basis for the guideline's upper bound of 10 years. This is not a resource-constrained compromise; it is the evidence-supported ceiling that the guideline's 5–10 range incorporates.

**Third, alignment with the guideline's GRADE strength.** Rec 8 is a conditional recommendation ("suggests"), not a strong recommendation ("recommends"). The conditional strength signals that the GDG considered the evidence sufficient to establish the range but not sufficient to commit to a point estimate. A country adaptation team is explicitly invited to resolve within the range on the basis of context. The 10-year resolution is within the range.

**Options not selected.** The 5-year lower bound (option `gp-lower-bound-5y`) is the evidentially-anchored European practice (von Karsa et al., 2015, cited in the IARC handbook) and would be the appropriate choice in a regime with *high existing coverage and no first-time-screenee backlog*. That is not the declared regime. The midpoint (7 years, `gp-midpoint-7y`) is arithmetic rather than evidential and is rejected on those grounds. The range-preserved option (`gp-range-preserved`) is the textualist's encoding and is not operationally actionable; preserving it in the purposive DMN would be an abdication of the purposive layer's purpose.

### WLHIV: resolved to every 5 years

The resolution for WLHIV is the upper bound of Rec 28's range: **every 5 years**. The reasoning is the same as for the general population — resource constraint under the declared regime, evidence of HPV screening's extended protective duration, conditional GRADE strength inviting country-level specification — with two WLHIV-specific modifications.

**First, the range itself is tighter.** Rec 28's range is 3–5 years, not 5–10. The WHO range already reflects the elevated risk of WLHIV (shorter intervals than the general population) and the guideline has already done the "higher risk → shorter interval" calibration within the WLHIV-specific recommendation. The 5-year resolution is the upper bound of an already-tightened range; it is not a casual lengthening.

**Second, WLHIV-specific post-treatment follow-up (Rec 33) remains shorter.** Rec 33 requires WLHIV post-treatment follow-up at 12 months with a second 12-month retest ("double follow-up"), and Rec 31 requires WLHIV triage-negative retest at 12 months. These WLHIV-specific retest burdens are not modified by cc-005. The 5-year resolution applies to *routine* rescreening for screen-negative or previously-negative WLHIV, not to the post-test retest cascade.

**Third, operational simplicity of unified interval.** Resolving WLHIV to 5 years produces an operationally simpler programme in jurisdictions where the general population is also at 5 years or 10 years: reminder systems, invitation registries, and facility-level coverage tracking can use a consistent rescreen cadence without branching by HIV status for the routine interval. (Branching by HIV status remains for age of screening onset (25 vs 30), the post-treatment follow-up path, and the triage requirement — this entry does not reduce those branches.)

**Options not selected.** The 3-year lower bound (`wlhiv-lower-bound-3y`) would be the appropriate choice if the binding constraint were incidence in WLHIV rather than first-time-screenee coverage, or in a jurisdiction with high existing coverage. It is not the declared regime. The midpoint (4 years, `wlhiv-midpoint-4y`) is arithmetic rather than evidential. The range-preserved option (`wlhiv-range-preserved`) is not operationally actionable.

### A tension the reader should see

This resolution lands at the upper bound of both ranges. That is a direction-of-travel outcome of the declared regime, not a general policy preference. A reader who disagrees with the resolution is most likely disagreeing with the regime rather than with this entry's reasoning within the regime. The appropriate place to lodge that disagreement is cc-000, not here. If the regime were reset — e.g., to a jurisdiction with high existing coverage and no first-time-screenee backlog — the analysis in this entry would likely flip to the lower bounds of both ranges.

This is a methodology feature, not a bug: specification entries should be derivable from the scope entry they depend on, so that a change in scope produces a traceable change in specification rather than a re-litigation of every downstream resolution. The register schema's requirement that specification entries cite evidence and regime together is what makes that traceability possible.

## Implementation Notes

### Purposive DMN changes

The purposive DMN (authored 2026-04-14) replaces the textualist's range-preserving outputs with the resolved values:

- `DecisionRule_1yry35i` (general population 30–49): output `"every 10 years"` in place of `"every 5 to 10 years"`.
- `DecisionRule_1925hkc` (WLHIV 25–49): output `"every 5 years"` in place of `"every 3 to 5 years"`.

The string output type is preserved on `OutputClause_0hq9puu` (`typeRef="string"`). A numeric-typeRef refactor was briefly considered but rejected as unnecessarily invasive; the string-replacement rewrite is sufficient.

### Two-bucket encoding in Due For Screening (revised 2026-04-14)

The first purposive DMN pass on 2026-04-14 kept the textualist's three-bucket structure in Due For Screening (`DecisionTable_1jr7lzl`), reframing the buckets around the resolved point estimates with a scheduling tolerance: `<10 / [10..12] / >12` for the general population and `<5 / [5..6] / >6` for WLHIV, emitting `"not yet due" / "within screening window" / "past screening window"` respectively. The tolerance windows (2-year for general, 1-year for WLHIV) were arbitrary and not grounded in either evidence or the cc-005 resolution.

On reflection the same afternoon, the three-bucket structure was collapsed to two buckets. The revised encoding is:

- `DecisionRule_1kowjor` (general population, not yet due): `<10`, emits `"not yet due"`
- `DecisionRule_1623wc6` (general population, at or past target): `>=10`, emits `"due"`
- `DecisionRule_0v20651` (WLHIV, not yet due): `<5`, emits `"not yet due"`
- `DecisionRule_0esotny` (WLHIV, at or past target): `>=5`, emits `"due"`
- (Past-window rules `DecisionRule_0d7mryk` and `DecisionRule_018yt1f` removed)

The `ScreeningStatus` output values declaration (`UnaryTests_1h5h3n3`) drops `"within screening window"` and `"past screening window"` in favour of a single `"due"` value. The remaining enum is: `"never screened — screen now"`, `"not yet due"`, `"due"`, `"prior positive — enter triage pathway"`, `"not eligible"`.

**Why the two-bucket encoding is preferable.** Three considerations converged against the tolerance-window approach:

1. **Faithfulness to the purposive intent.** cc-005's resolution is a point estimate (10 years, 5 years), not a range. The tolerance windows quietly re-introduced range thinking through the back door: the three buckets implicitly re-encoded an interval (`[10..12]`) where the resolution says "one value." A two-bucket encoding is honest about what the resolution says.

2. **Removal of arbitrary parameters.** The 2-year general-population tolerance and the 1-year WLHIV tolerance had no evidential or regime basis. They were author judgement calls made in a minimum-change spirit during the first purposive pass. Removing them removes a layer of undocumented interpretation.

3. **Downstream is unaffected.** `ScreeningStatus` is a terminal output of Due For Screening. Nothing in the cascade (Triage, Ablation, Treatment, Follow-up) consumes it. External systems that want to distinguish "just-barely due" from "substantially overdue" have `YearsSinceLastScreen` as a raw numeric input and can compute urgency themselves. The three-bucket label was a pre-computation that the DAK did not need to perform.

**What a reader looking for the "past window" signal should know.** The DAK no longer emits a distinct `"past screening window"` label. A woman who has not been screened in 25 years receives `"due"` just as a woman who is exactly at her 10-year mark does. If a downstream workflow needs to prioritise overdue cases, it queries `YearsSinceLastScreen` directly rather than pattern-matching on `ScreeningStatus`. This is a deliberate trade between signal granularity at the DAK output layer and downstream flexibility at the scheduling layer; the DAK's job is to say whether screening is indicated, and the scheduling layer's job is to say when and in what order.

**Register authoring note.** This revision is the kind of second-pass refinement the purposive layer is designed to permit. The first pass made the safer minimum-change move; the second pass made the cleaner move once the implications were visible. This entry is revised in place (with `revised: 2026-04-14`) rather than superseded, because the core resolution — 10 years and 5 years at the upper bounds — is unchanged. Only the downstream encoding of that resolution has been refined.

### Downstream rules that carry interval-sensitive outputs

The textualist also contains literal interval strings in outputs of later decisions — specifically the Triage Decision's "repeat HPV test in 2 years" outputs (lines 441, 623, 629, 741, 747 of the textualist). These are *not* the same interval concept as the Rec 8 / Rec 28 routine rescreen. They are the Rec 11 (24-month retest for triage-negative general population) and Rec 31 (12-month retest for triage-negative WLHIV) retest windows, and they are operationalised correctly in the textualist as 2-year (general population) literals. The WLHIV 12-month retest is a separate textualist fidelity issue already flagged in [`index.md`](index.md) "Deferred and out of scope" (the "double follow-up" gap). This entry does not modify them.

### When this entry would need revision

This entry should be re-examined if:

1. The epidemiological regime in cc-000 is revised — particularly the first-time-screenee backlog assumption. If future data shows existing coverage rising substantially, the resource-constraint argument weakens and the lower bounds become more attractive.
2. A future WHO guideline revision tightens the ranges or provides point estimates. The 2021 conditional recommendations were based on low-certainty evidence; higher-certainty evidence in a later revision could narrow or replace the range, at which point this entry should harmonise with the revised guideline rather than continuing to resolve the older range.
3. HPV vaccination coverage in the aging cohort rises to the point where the unvaccinated-cohort assumption inherited from 2021-07 no longer holds. Vaccinated-cohort intervals are an evidence-active area (see `cc-004` when drafted) and may eventually warrant longer routine intervals than the current range contemplates.

The `evidence_volatile` flag is false on this entry because the *interval resolution* is not itself evidence-volatile in the sense cc-002 and cc-004 are: the HPV screening interval evidence base is reasonably stable. What is regime-sensitive is the *choice of bound within the range*, and that sensitivity is documented in the "tension the reader should see" section of the rationale rather than as an evidence-volatility flag.
