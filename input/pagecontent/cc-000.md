## What this entry is

Unlike other register entries, this one does not document a single interpretive act in the translation from the guideline to the DMN. It declares the scope within which all other interpretive acts take place. It is the entry a reader should see first, and every subsequent entry should be understood as operating within the scope this entry sets.

## What the DAK models

The cervical cancer DAK, as currently authored, models **Algorithm 5** from the WHO 2021 guideline (p.68): HPV DNA primary screening with visual inspection with acetic acid (VIA) as the triage step, for HPV-positive women, with ablation or excision treatment for eligible lesions and referral for suspected invasive cancer. The cascade modelled by the textualist DMN consists of six decisions:

1. Eligible For Screening (by age, sex/anatomy, HIV status)
2. Due for Screening (by time since last screen, prior result, interval)
3. Triage Decision (HPV-negative → rescreen; HPV-positive → VIA; VIA result → treatment pathway)
4. Ablation Eligibility (by transformation zone type and extent)
5. Treatment Decision (ablation, excision, or referral)
6. Follow-up Decision (post-treatment retest schedule, modulated by population and treatment type)

The DAK models the decisions themselves — the logic of the cascade — and takes as given the clinical data on which the decisions operate (age, HIV status, HPV DNA result, VIA finding, transformation zone type). It does not model the acquisition of that data, the workflow around collecting it, or the upstream clinical judgments (e.g., what counts as VIA-positive, what counts as "cancer suspected") that populate the inputs.

## What the DAK does not model

Several things are explicitly out of scope. Omitting them is not an oversight; it is a scoping choice, and readers (particularly country adaptation teams) should know the choice has been made before they try to deploy the DAK.

**HPV vaccination decisions.** The WHO Cervical Cancer Elimination Strategy is a three-part program — 90% of girls vaccinated by age 15, 70% of women screened by 35 and 45, 90% of women with positive results treated (the "90-70-90 targets"). This DAK models the middle leg, in its Algorithm 5 form, only. Vaccination eligibility, schedule, catch-up, and tracking are entirely out of scope. A vaccination decision-support DAK is a separate artifact that should be authored separately and that would reference this DAK only at the boundaries (e.g., where vaccination history becomes an input to screening decisions — see `cc-004`).

**Alternative screening algorithms and the selection of Algorithm 5.** The 2021 guideline presents seven possible screening algorithms, corresponding to different combinations of primary test (HPV DNA, VIA, cytology), triage strategy (VIA, cytology, dual-stain, genotyping, colposcopy), and treatment pathway. The algorithms form a gradient of infrastructure demand: Algorithm 1 (VIA screen-and-treat) requires no laboratory at all; Algorithms 2 and 5 require HPV DNA testing; Algorithms 3, 6, and 7 require cytology labs and/or colposcopy; Algorithm 4 requires HPV16/18 genotyping capability. A country that deploys VIA-only screening (Algorithm 1), HPV self-sampling with direct-to-treatment, or co-testing needs a different DAK. The choice among algorithms is itself an evidence-informed decision that depends on local infrastructure, workforce capacity, and health-system context, and it is made upstream of this DAK by the country adaptation team, not by the DAK.

Algorithm 5 was chosen for this case study as a moderate-complexity exercise of the methodology. It requires a laboratory capability (HPV DNA) and a clinical skill (VIA triage) but not colposcopy or cytology, placing it in the middle of the infrastructure gradient. Its two-step cascade (screen then triage) exercises more register entries than Algorithm 1 (screen-and-treat, no triage step) while remaining simpler than Algorithm 4 (genotyping branching with VIA fallback). These are legitimate reasons for the choice, but the selection should be read with awareness of the bias it carries. The countries with the highest cervical cancer burden — particularly in East and Southern Africa — are in many cases still deploying Algorithm 1 (VIA screen-and-treat) because HPV DNA testing is not yet available at scale in their facilities. A methodology exercise that models only Algorithm 5 produces artifacts that the highest-need settings cannot use until their laboratory infrastructure catches up. Algorithm 1 would have been the choice that confronts the hardest deployment reality.

This bias does not invalidate the case study. The methodology's value is in the register structure, the interpretive discipline, and the authoring workflow, all of which transfer to any algorithm. But the bias should be visible to a reader — particularly a country adaptation team considering whether this case study's artifacts are relevant to their context — rather than silently inherited.

**Reach, equity, and the upstream barriers to screening access.** The DAK models the in-facility decisions once a woman has arrived at a screening encounter. It does not model — and cannot model — the problems that determine whether she arrives at all: distance from a facility, transportation cost, childcare and time-off barriers, awareness of screening services, provider referral behaviour, linkage between HIV care and screening services, and the structural exclusions affecting rural women, adolescents, sex workers, migrants, and refugees. In many target deployments these upstream problems are the binding constraint on cervical cancer outcomes, and a DAK that models the cascade perfectly and ignores reach has not solved the cervical cancer problem. This is not a criticism of the DAK — decision-support for the in-facility cascade is a useful artifact and solving reach is a different kind of work — but it is a scope boundary that should be declared rather than assumed.

**Clinical judgments that populate the boolean inputs.** The DAK takes `CancerSuspected`, `VIAResult`, `HPVResult`, and `TransformationZoneType` as given. Behind each of these is a clinical determination made by a screening nurse or clinician, often under time pressure, with variable training and without routine second-opinion support. In particular, the `CancerSuspected` determination is known to have substantial inter-observer variability, and its downstream consequences (ablation vs excision vs referral) are serious in either direction. See `cc-003` for the register entry documenting this specifically. The DAK assumes these judgments are made competently and does not model their upstream provenance.

**Treatment delivery and complications.** The DAK models the *decision* about treatment modality (ablation, excision, referral) but not the *delivery* of treatment, the tracking of complications, the management of anaesthesia (for cold-knife conization, though not for thermal ablation or LLETZ), or the supply-chain considerations around consumables. A country deploying the DAK still needs separate workflow support for these.

**Data quality, completeness, and missing-data handling at the population level.** The DAK's catch-all rules handle null inputs at the individual level (e.g., null age returns ineligible-for-screening), but the DAK does not model what happens when a whole facility has systematically missing data for some field, or when a data migration has introduced errors, or when prior screening records exist on paper but not in the EHR. These are real problems in many deployment contexts and they belong in operational quality assurance, not in the DAK.

## Assumptions inherited from the 2021 guideline

Some assumptions the DAK makes are inherited from the source guideline without being re-examined in this case study. Naming them helps a future reviewer identify where interpretive work might still be needed.

**Unvaccinated-cohort assumption.** The 2021 guideline was written when the first fully HPV-vaccinated cohorts were still below screening age in most countries. As a result, the guideline's recommendations for screening age, interval, and triage are implicitly calibrated to an unvaccinated or vaccination-status-unknown population. This assumption is becoming less defensible as HPV vaccination coverage rises in screening-age cohorts in increasing numbers of countries. The DAK inherits the assumption without modification; `cc-004` is the register entry that documents the gap and proposes a structural change to accept vaccination status as an input when the guidance catches up.

**Sexual exposure precondition.** The 2021 guideline assumes prior sexual exposure as an implicit precondition for screening, operationalised via the age thresholds (screening starts at 25 for WLHIV or 30 for general population, ages by which most women have had sufficient time since sexual debut for meaningful HPV exposure and progression). The DAK inherits this assumption and does not model sexual history as an input. The small population of never-sexually-active individuals above the age threshold is therefore over-screened under this DAK. `cc-007` is the register entry that documents this as a deliberate simplification.

**Patient-level HIV status as sufficient for risk stratification.** The guideline recommends a stricter regime for women living with HIV (WLHIV) — earlier onset, shorter interval, modified triage and follow-up — and encodes "living with HIV" as a patient-level boolean. The DAK inherits this encoding. The evidence base is increasingly moving toward finer-grained HIV-status stratification (CD4 count, viral suppression duration, ART adherence), but neither the 2021-12 mRNA addendum nor the 2024-06 dual-stain cytology addendum makes a finer-grained WLHIV recommendation — the 2024-06 publication explicitly defers WLHIV recommendations on grounds of insufficient evidence. `cc-002` is the register entry that documents the current boolean encoding and flags the evolution of the evidence base.

**Single-visit vs multi-visit cascade.** The Algorithm 5 cascade as authored in the textualist treats screening, triage, treatment, and follow-up as distinct encounters. In real deployments, particularly in resource-constrained rural settings, many countries compress the cascade into a single visit ("screen-and-treat") to avoid loss-to-follow-up between encounters. The DAK's decision logic is compatible with single-visit workflows — the decisions still fire in the same order — but the DAK does not model the workflow choice explicitly. A country deploying single-visit screen-and-treat should be aware that the DAK's `Follow-up Decision` table assumes the treatment has been completed and the next encounter is a follow-up, which is the same assumption the guideline makes.

**"Women" as the population language.** The guideline's formal recommendation language uses "women" throughout. An implementation guidance note in Section 3.1 (p.21) extends the target population to "transgender men and non-binary and intersex individuals who have a cervix." The split is a deliberate compromise reflecting the evidence base: the HPV screening trials that underpin the recommendations enrolled cisgender women, and GRADE fidelity limits what Recommendation 1 can say about populations not represented in the evidence. See `cc-001` for the register entry documenting the interpretive question and its provisional resolution.

## Epidemiological regime this case study assumes

Per `index.md`, the policy posture of the hypothetical jurisdiction inhabited by this case study is not context-free. It is conditional on an epidemiological regime that must be named so that subsequent `specification` entries (particularly `cc-005` on screening interval) can justify their resolutions consistently. The regime is declared here so it has a single authoritative location.

**Baseline cervical cancer incidence.** Moderate to high. The jurisdiction has age-standardised incidence in the range where cervical cancer is among the leading cancers of women and a serious public health concern — roughly comparable to the East and Southern African context. This is not a low-incidence setting where the population-level benefit of screening approaches the low-yield false-positive regime; it is a setting where aggressive screening is clinically defensible on outcomes grounds.

**HIV prevalence in the screening-age population.** Moderate to high. A meaningful fraction of women presenting for screening will be living with HIV, and the WLHIV-specific regime in the guideline will be operationally relevant for a substantial portion of the cascade. `cc-002` will need to account for this.

**HPV vaccination coverage in the aging cohort.** Low to moderate. The jurisdiction has introduced HPV vaccination but the first cohorts have not yet aged into screening eligibility in large numbers, so the "unvaccinated cohort" assumption inherited from the 2021 guideline is still approximately valid. This will not remain the case indefinitely; `cc-004` documents the DAK's non-handling of vaccination status as an acknowledged future gap rather than a permanent design choice.

**Existing screening coverage and first-time-screen backlog.** Low to moderate. Most women of screening age in the jurisdiction have not yet been screened, and the binding resource constraint on the screening program is reaching first-time-screenees rather than rescreening previously-negative ones. **This matters for `cc-005` (interval resolution) in a specific way**: under a fixed screening budget, shortening the rescreen interval competes with first-time-screen coverage, and in a low-coverage regime the evidence-informed policy choice may well be the longer bound of the guideline's range rather than the shorter. The `index.md` earlier talked about an "aggressive posture" as a default; this scope entry makes the posture conditional and notes that in the current regime the "aggressive" and "equitable" directions may actually be in tension.

**Health system capacity.** The jurisdiction has functioning public-sector primary care with some EHR adoption (typically for HIV care and some gynaecology modules), DHIS2-based aggregate reporting, and an intermittent but real capability to perform HPV DNA testing at central or regional laboratories with sample transport from peripheral facilities. It does not have FHIR `BodyStructure` resources, anatomical inventory tracking, or systematic hysterectomy-history capture. This matters for `cc-001` in particular: the DAK's input vocabulary needs to be expressible against the EHR fields that actually exist, not against an idealised FHIR model. See `cc-001`'s revised rationale for the consequences.

**Workforce capacity for VIA and treatment delivery.** Moderate. VIA triage is performed by nurses with varied training and limited routine quality assurance. The inter-observer variability of VIA is a known limitation of Algorithm 5 in this regime, and `cc-003` ("cancer suspected" clinical judgment) is the register entry that documents the DAK's handling of this.

This regime is not claimed to be epidemiologically rigorous or to represent a specific real country. It is a coherent set of preconditions that let the register's `specification` entries make consistent choices. A different regime would produce different consistent choices; the methodology does not privilege this one except as an exercise.

## Addendum reconciliation status

Three WHO cervical cancer documents sit behind this DAK: the 2021-07 guideline (Algorithm 5 source), the 2021-12 mRNA addendum, and the 2024-06 dual-stain cytology addendum. 

**WHO June 2024 addendum — not a blocker.** The 2024-06 publication is scoped strictly to dual-stain cytology (p16/Ki-67) as a triage test for the general population of women. It does not touch VIA triage (which is Algorithm 5's triage step). It makes no WLHIV recommendation (it explicitly defers on grounds of insufficient evidence, and its Annex 5 reprints the 2021 WLHIV recommendation unchanged). It makes no HPV vaccination recommendation. It is therefore not a blocker for `cc-002`, `cc-004`, or `cc-005`. It is retained as narrative context for the evidence-volatility framing around triage strategies (`cc-003`).

**WHO December 2021 mRNA addendum — reconciliation complete.** The 2024-06 publication reprints its 12-month post-treatment follow-up flow (Annex 4, page 42) citing the 2021-12 mRNA addendum as the source, which raised the question whether 2021-12 had modified the post-treatment follow-up algorithm that was in 2021-07 for the HPV DNA + VIA path. The narrow reconciliation is documented at [`addendum-2021-12-reconciliation.md` (see [source repo](https://github.com/dhes/dak-authoring-methodology/blob/main/case-studies/cervical-cancer/narrative/addendum-2021-12-reconciliation.md))](https://github.com/dhes/dak-authoring-methodology/blob/main/case-studies/cervical-cancer/narrative/addendum-2021-12-reconciliation.md). Findings: 2021-12 adds HPV mRNA as an alternative primary screening test for the general population only, with 5-year intervals, with or without triage; its Chapter 3 makes no changes to any 2021-07 recommendation; its Annex 5 reprints Rec 13 (general-population post-treatment follow-up) and Rec 33 (WLHIV "double follow-up") verbatim; its Annex 4 p.44 reprints the 2021-07 p.71 general-population post-treatment follow-up flow with only minor phrasing generalization to accommodate mRNA's 5-year interval; and it does not reprint the 2021-07 p.72 WLHIV-specific post-treatment follow-up flow at all. No textualist DMN correction is required on account of 2021-12. A pre-existing WLHIV "double follow-up" fidelity gap in the textualist was confirmed during the reconciliation — it originates in the 2021-07 reading, not in any addendum, and is tracked as a future register-entry candidate (see `index.md` "Deferred and out of scope").

## This entry is not flagged

Unlike other entries, this one does not have a `flagged-for-sme-review` status or a provisional encoding. Scope declarations are always active: they describe what the DAK is for and what it is not for, and a reviewer can disagree with the scope but the scope still applies to the current artifact. A disagreement with the scope would be resolved by either rewriting the DAK (to expand scope) or by writing a separate DAK (to address the out-of-scope items), not by changing this entry.

If future WHO publications, ongoing living-guideline updates, or future clinical input reveal that the inherited assumptions (unvaccinated cohort, patient-level HIV boolean, sexual exposure precondition, "women" population language, Algorithm 5 as the modelled algorithm) are no longer tenable even as inherited defaults, this entry should be updated to document the change in scope rather than absorbed into a new entry. Scope changes are major revisions of the register; they are not routine.
