## First case study decision (2026-04-11)

**Selected for the first case study:** `sex-female`. **Deferred to a future purposive refinement:** `has-cervix`. **Status:** accepted for the first case study; the `has-cervix` purposive refinement remains documented but is no longer blocking any downstream work.

The decision to accept `Sex = Female` as the first-case-study encoding was made on 2026-04-11 during the authoring of the minimum-viable Core Data Dictionary at [`../data-dictionary/`](../data-dictionary/). Earlier drafts of this entry — including the version immediately preceding this one — held the choice open as `flagged-for-sme-review` with `has-cervix` as the provisional encoding in the (then-unauthored) purposive DMN. That framing was the right framing *before* the first case study had made contact with real harvested CDD content and with the field literature on country adaptation. It became the wrong framing once three new considerations aligned:

**1. Textualist/CDD consistency.** The textualist DMN's [Eligible For Screening decision](../textualist.dmn) uses `ClientIsWoman` as its first input, faithful to the 2021-07 guideline Recommendation 1 language "women." The first-case-study CDD documents what the textualist actually consumes, not a hypothetical purposive refinement. A CDD row describing `HasCervix` as the population element would be a row describing something that does not yet exist in the DMN. That mismatch would defeat the CDD's purpose, which is to be the L2 face of the decision logic as it currently stands.

**2. Muliokela premapping test.** The methodology now positions the L2/L3 column boundary as tri-purpose — layer separation, cross-DAK harmonization, and country-adaptation workflow (see [`../../../methodology/authoring-workflow.md`](../../../methodology/authoring-workflow.md) subsection "The boundary is tri-purpose, not layer-only"). The country-adaptation purpose is grounded in Muliokela et al. (2025), whose Premapping Tool asks of each DAK data element: "does it already exist in the country's protocols/guidelines/registers, should it be added, does it need modifications?" Applied to the two candidates:

   - `Sex = Female` passes the premapping test in every one of Muliokela's five pathfinder countries (Ethiopia, Ghana, Malawi, Zambia, Zimbabwe). Every LMIC public-sector EHR and HMIS has a sex field. Every paper register has a sex field. Every workflow is accustomed to populating it. The premapping answer is "yes, exists; no modification needed."
   - `HasCervix` fails the premapping test in every one of those countries. No deployment-target EHR in the public-sector landscape (Bahmni, eTracker, SmartCare, Impilo, iHRIS, OpenMRS-based systems, DHIS2) carries `BodyStructure` or anatomical-inventory fields. The premapping answer is "does not exist; needs substantial upstream data-model work to add; none of the current vendors have committed to that work."

   A first-case-study CDD that asks countries to populate a field their EHR cannot express is aspirational architecture at the cost of deployment reality. The methodology's point is to respect deployment reality, not to override it for aesthetic reasons.

**3. GDG compromise respect.** Almonte's clarification in the April 2026 simulated review established that the 2021-07 Rec 1 / Section 3.1 split was a deliberate negotiated compromise, not an inconsistency: Rec 1 uses "women" because the GRADE evidence base (HPV primary screening trials enrolled predominantly cisgender women) limits what the formal recommendation can assert, while Section 3.1 implementation guidance extends to transgender men and non-binary / intersex individuals with a cervix because the clinical rationale for screening applies regardless of gender identity. The compromise split the evidential responsibility across two epistemic layers within the same guideline document. The textualist mirrors Rec 1 — the higher-stringency layer — and the CDD mirroring the textualist is the faithful reading. Accepting `Sex = Female` for the first case study is respecting the GDG compromise, not dissolving it.

**What is not being decided.** The `has-cervix` encoding is not withdrawn from the methodology's thinking. It remains documented in the [CDD's "Deferred as purposive refinement" section](../data-dictionary/cervical-cancer-cdd.md) as the case study's candidate contribution to a future smart-core Kind-3 entry. Two conditions would revive it as a live row:

1. A future purposive DMN authoring pass chooses to encode the Section 3.1 broader population at the DMN level, renaming `ClientIsWoman` → `HasCervix` and carrying the anatomical criterion into the decision logic. At that point this entry's status would change to "accepted (first case study, Sex); accepted (purposive, HasCervix)" with the CDD gaining a row 20 for `HasCervix` alongside the existing row 2 for `Sex`.
2. A Phase B smart-core-candidates triangulation artifact (per the plan file at `silly-gathering-flamingo.md` in the user's local Claude state, documented but not committed to the project repo) picks up `HasCervix` as a proposed-to-core element, at which point the CDD would add it as a Kind-3 row with a pointer to the candidate artifact.

Neither condition is met as of 2026-04-11. The deferral is honest — the work is documented, named, and reachable — and it is not urgent because the first case study is deployable under `Sex = Female` without functional limitation.

**What the named review venue is still for.** The HRP coordination meeting (with clinical informatician input from an active Algorithm 5 deployment) named in the frontmatter is no longer blocking the first case study. Its role is narrowed to: *if a future purposive refinement pursues the `has-cervix` encoding, or if a Phase B triangulation artifact proposes `HasCervix` to smart-core, the HRP venue is where the clinical-informatician adjudication of the encoding should happen.* The review-by date (2026-10-01) applies to that narrower question, not to the first-case-study decision.

**What this decision closes.** The following downstream items that were blocked on `cc-001` being unsettled can now proceed:

- The CC case study's minimum-viable CDD — authored on 2026-04-11, using this decision as its population-element row.
- The methodology's tri-purpose L2/L3 boundary subsection in `authoring-workflow.md` — authored on 2026-04-11, using this decision as the exemplar of the Muliokela premapping test in action.
- Any future register entries that depend on the population element being settled (none currently; `cc-002`, `cc-003`, `cc-004` are independent of this decision).

## Rationale (historical; records the reasoning that led to the first case study decision above)

The rationale below records the four-axis reasoning that was in force when this entry was `flagged-for-sme-review` with `has-cervix` as the provisional encoding. It is retained in full because it is the evidence the first case study decision above is built on. A reader encountering this entry for the first time should read the decision block above first, then read this rationale to understand how the decision was reached.

This entry previously had no `selected` option. The choice was held open pending review by the venue identified in the frontmatter, and the purposive DMN was to use `has-cervix` provisionally. The policy-context posture declared in `index.md` (the hypothetical jurisdiction's posture, conditional on the epidemiological regime declared in `cc-000`) does not apply here — there is no aggressive/conservative axis on this question — so the fallback framing governed.

The rationale below is structured as four considerations: fidelity to the guideline (with Almonte's correction on what the GDG compromise actually was), L3 implementability (with Leitner's correction on how large the gap actually is), deployment reality (Barango's point about LMIC EHRs and Bloem's point about pragmatic vaccination-program practice), and why the entry was flagged rather than decided at that earlier point. In the 2026-04-11 reassessment, the fidelity and deployment-reality axes converged on `sex-female` as the first-case-study answer, and the L3 implementability and "why flagged" arguments are now mostly of historical interest.

### Fidelity axis — corrected

An earlier draft of this entry treated Recommendation 1 and Section 3.1 as near-peer statements of the guideline and concluded that `has-cervix` dominates on fidelity because it is consistent with both. That framing is incomplete and, in the framing of the April 2026 simulated review, partially wrong. The correction has two parts.

First, Mehl's point: Rec 1 and Section 3.1 are **not epistemically equal in WHO's own hierarchy**. Recommendations in a WHO guideline are the output of a GRADE process (systematic evidence review, external Guidelines Development Group, executive sign-off, evidence-profile appraisal), and carry institutional weight accordingly. Implementation guidance in the narrative sections of the same document is contributed by the guideline writing group as supporting material; it is helpful, it is considered, but it has not been through the same evidence appraisal and it is subordinate to the recommendation language. An L2 that treats the two as peer statements is making a category error about WHO's own epistemics.

Second, Almonte's clarification — which corrects both the earlier draft and Mehl's framing. The split between "women" in Rec 1 and the broader population language in Section 3.1 was **not an oversight by the implementation guidance authors**, and Section 3.1 is **not subordinate in the sense of being less considered**. The split was a deliberate GDG compromise at the time the 2021 guideline was finalised. The argument for keeping "women" in Rec 1 was evidential: the HPV primary screening trials that underpin the recommendation enrolled predominantly cisgender women, and GRADE fidelity limits what Rec 1 can say about populations not meaningfully represented in the trial data. The argument for including the broader language somewhere in the guideline was clinical: the rationale for cervical cancer screening (exposure of a cervix to HPV) applies regardless of gender identity, and omitting the broader population would be a failure of the guideline to speak to its full target audience. The compromise was to put the evidence-calibrated population language in the formal recommendation and the clinically-rational broader population language in implementation guidance at lower evidential stringency. HRP and advocacy partners were involved in drafting the Section 3.1 language; it was not a footnote.

The implication for this entry's fidelity analysis: the split is **a deliberate, evidence-calibrated tension, not an inconsistency to be resolved by picking one side**. A purposive L2 that chooses `gender = woman` is faithful to the recommendation language but fails to speak to the Section 3.1 population, which the guideline *does* speak to. A purposive L2 that chooses `has-cervix` aligns with Section 3.1's clinical rationale and the guideline's direction of travel, but encodes a claim (the broader population) at a higher stringency than the trial evidence currently supports. Neither is simply wrong. Both are positions within a documented WHO-internal compromise.

The most honest L2 position is probably to acknowledge the tension explicitly in the register (which is what this entry now does) and to track it as an item for the next guideline revision cycle, when accumulating evidence from more representative trials may let the recommendation language itself broaden and dissolve the compromise. This makes the resolution of cc-001 a **flag for upstream guidance evolution**, not a jurisdictional policy question about which reading to privilege.

### L3 implementability axis — corrected

The earlier draft argued that `has-cervix` is "substantially more work" than `gender = woman` at the L3 layer, framing the cost as "one FHIRPath expression versus a CQL library." Leitner's pushback in the April 2026 simulated review is that this framing is misleading in two ways.

First, the claim that `has-cervix` requires "a CQL library" overstates the size of the work. The actual CQL for a has-cervix determination — a `define` expression querying `BodyStructure` for cervix presence, a fallback query over hysterectomy history, a null-handling clause, and a return — is on the order of ten to twenty lines. It is comparable in scale to CQL already shipped in the SMART Guidelines libraries for queries like "most recent pregnancy status," "prior delivery history," or "active immunization." These are routine queries in the ecosystem; they are not considered heroic and they are not libraries.

Second, the per-DAK implementability framing ignores the Core Data Dictionary architecture. Terminology bindings, missing-data semantics, and FHIR-profile choices for data elements like "has a cervix" are not things each DAK solves independently. They are solved once, at the Core Data Dictionary level, and every DAK that needs the element references it by canonical URI. Once the Core Data Dictionary has a `HasCervix` element (or finds that an existing adjacent element — e.g., from the HIV or maternal health DAKs — can be reused), the per-DAK cost of using it is close to zero. The costly work is done once and amortised.

Third, and more important for the methodology itself: Mehl's layer-confusion concern. Weighing CQL cost in the L2 register is letting the L3 implementation shape the L2 interpretation, which is the anti-pattern the textualist-purposive split exists to prevent. The honest position is that L3 implementability **should not drive the L2 interpretive choice**. If the choice is `gender = woman`, it should be because that is the right interpretive answer to the population-definition question, not because it is cheaper in CQL. If the choice is `has-cervix`, it should be on clinical/evidential grounds, not because the anatomical query is architecturally interesting.

What remains of the implementability axis after these corrections: L3 concerns are still worth recording as annotations for L3 implementers, but they do not weigh on the interpretive choice. The annotation belongs in the implementation notes section, not in the rationale for flagging the entry. That is where it now sits.

### Deployment reality axis (new)

A consideration that the earlier draft missed entirely, added now per Barango's and Bloem's contributions to the April 2026 simulated review.

**Most target deployments cannot implement `has-cervix` even if they choose to.** Barango's direct observation: in the fifteen East and Southern African countries he supports, the public-sector EHR landscape consists of paper registers, DHIS2 aggregate reporting, and point-of-care EMRs (SmartCare, OpenMRS-based systems, IQCare) that have a patient sex field and may or may not capture hysterectomy history in gynaecology modules. None of them have FHIR `BodyStructure` resources or anatomical inventory concepts. A purposive L2 that canonicalises `HasCervix` as its input produces an artifact that most target countries cannot deploy without substantial upstream data-model work. In practice, the deployment answer in most of these contexts will be **`sex = female` with the Section 3.1 population served by targeted outreach in a parallel workflow**, because that is what the data layer supports. The DAK's interpretive answer should be honest about this: the `has-cervix` option is not simply "the right answer that is more expensive to implement"; for most deployments it is "an answer the data layer cannot express at all."

**HPV vaccination programs in the same countries already use sex-at-birth pragmatically without controversy.** Bloem's point: school-based HPV vaccination delivery to "girls aged 9 to 14, defined by sex at birth" has been operational for more than a decade in over a hundred countries, and the target population of transgender men and non-binary individuals with a cervix is reached by separate outreach mechanisms where they exist at all. This is not a recommendation for how screening should work — screening is a different clinical scenario, the data systems are different, and the harms of missing someone at screening age are different from the harms of missing a 12-year-old who can be caught on a later vaccination round. But it is a reminder that the sex/gender/anatomy question is being answered pragmatically by upstream programs in the same health systems, and a screening DAK that insists on a different data model than the vaccination program running in the same country is a screening DAK that will not integrate with the other cervical cancer artifacts in that country's deployment.

**Composite effect on the analysis.** Deployment reality does not settle the interpretive question (it is not supposed to; deployment reality is about what the L3 can do, not about what the L2 should say), but it substantially shifts what is at stake. The fidelity question Almonte clarified is a question for the next WHO guideline revision cycle. The implementability question Leitner corrected is smaller than the earlier draft claimed. The deployment reality question is where most of the practical weight actually sits: for most target jurisdictions in the intended user community (country adaptation teams, per `index.md` and the findings memo), the DAK will be deployed with `sex = female` regardless of how the register resolves the interpretive question, because that is what the data layer supports.

### Why `disambiguation` not `inference`, and why not `correction`

The guideline takes positions on both sides of the split — Rec 1 uses "women," Section 3.1 extends the population — so this is not an inference over a silence. The interpretive act is between two positions the guideline holds simultaneously.

Mehl proposed recategorising the entry as `correction` on the grounds that Rec 1 and Section 3.1 are inconsistent. Almonte's clarification shows that they are not inconsistent in the sense of being unreconciled — the GDG reconciled them explicitly, by splitting the evidential responsibility between a recommendation and an implementation note. The tension is deliberate, documented, and actively being worked on for the next revision cycle. `correction` would mischaracterise this as a guideline error when it is in fact a negotiated settlement.

So the category stays as `disambiguation`, but with the clarified understanding that the disambiguation is between two layers of the same guideline document that were deliberately assigned different epistemic statuses, not between two contradictory readings of a single statement.

### Why the entry was flagged at the April 2026 simulated review, and why it is no longer

At the April 2026 simulated review, putting the four considerations together produced this reading:

- Fidelity: neither `has-cervix` nor `gender = woman` is simply faithful. The compromise is deliberate and the tension is upstream.
- L3 implementability: smaller gap than the earlier draft claimed, and in any case should not drive the interpretive choice per Mehl.
- Deployment reality: for most target jurisdictions, the data layer forces `sex = female` regardless of the register's interpretive answer.
- Review venue: the decision belongs in a named venue (HRP coordination with clinical informatician input from an active deployment), not with the methodology author.

The honest move at that time was to record the considerations, keep the entry `flagged-for-sme-review` with a named venue and a `review-by` date, and let the review venue adjudicate. The provisional encoding in the (not-yet-authored) purposive DMN was `has-cervix` on direction-of-travel grounds, with the expectation that a reviewer might well flip it to `sex = female` on deployment-reality grounds.

**What changed between 2026-04-09 and 2026-04-11.** The deployment-reality argument in the third bullet above always pointed at `sex = female` as the actual deployment encoding. Between April 9 (the simulated review) and April 11 (the minimum-viable CDD authoring pass), the methodology gained two artifacts that made the deployment-reality argument load-bearing rather than advisory: (a) the connection to Muliokela et al. (2025)'s pathfinder-country premapping workflow, which elevates "does the element exist in the country's EHR?" from a consideration to the core test of whether a DAK data element is country-adaptation-ready; and (b) the tri-purpose framing of the L2/L3 boundary, which positions the country-adaptation purpose as equally weighty to the layer-separation and cross-DAK purposes rather than as an afterthought. With those two artifacts in hand, the "let the review venue decide" posture stopped being the honest move and started being a deferral of a question whose answer was already visible in the work that had been done. The first case study decision block near the top of this entry records the resolution.

The review venue (HRP coordination meeting) is still named for any future reconsideration of `has-cervix` as a purposive refinement, but it is no longer blocking anything. The case study, the methodology, and the CDD all proceed on `sex-female` as of 2026-04-11.

## Implementation Notes

### First case study DMN encoding (and deferred purposive refinement)

**First case study — textualist.** The textualist DMN continues to use `ClientIsWoman` as the population-element input in the Eligible For Screening decision, faithful to 2021-07 Recommendation 1 language "women." No change to the textualist on account of this entry. The Core Data Dictionary row that documents this input is [`../data-dictionary/cervical-cancer-cdd.md`](../data-dictionary/cervical-cancer-cdd.md) row 2 (`Sex`, Kind 2, harvested from `HIV.A.DE25`), with the mapping `ClientIsWoman = (Sex = Female)` recorded in that row's `Terminology Intent` column.

**First case study — purposive.** No purposive DMN has yet been authored. If and when one is, it should continue to use the textualist's `ClientIsWoman` input, aligned with the CDD's row 2 `Sex` element, for the three reasons in the first-case-study decision block above.

**Deferred purposive refinement — `HasCervix`.** A future purposive DMN authoring pass may choose to pursue the Section 3.1 broader population by renaming `ClientIsWoman` → `HasCervix` throughout the Eligible For Screening decision table. All rules currently keyed on `ClientIsWoman = true/false/null` would become keyed on `HasCervix = true/false/null`; no rule structure change (this is a rename plus a semantic shift). At the same time, the CDD would gain a new row (candidate row 20) for `HasCervix` (Kind 1 + Kind 3, novel to CC and proposed-to-core) alongside the existing row 2 for `Sex`, and row 2 would remain as the textualist's input row.

The decision to pursue the `HasCervix` refinement is not bundled into this entry. Conditions under which it might be revisited:

1. A Core Data Dictionary proposal for a `HasCervix` element lands successfully in smart-core (or a successor venue), so the L3 binding can be consumed rather than authored locally. The Phase B triangulation artifact documented in the plan file (`silly-gathering-flamingo.md` in the user's local Claude state) is the methodology's proposed path to generating that proposal, but it is explicitly deferred until the CC CDD first pass is validated.
2. A future WHO guideline revision resolves the Rec 1 / Section 3.1 split upstream, at which point the DAK should harmonise with whatever the revised recommendation language says. Per Almonte's observation, this is the most likely resolution in the medium term as more representative trial evidence accumulates.
3. A specific deployment context requires the broader population encoding for operational reasons (e.g., a jurisdiction with an active trans-health program that wants the DAK to explicitly support its target population), at which point a deployment-specific purposive refinement could be authored with `HasCervix` as its population element.

None of these conditions is met as of 2026-04-11, and none is blocking the first case study. The review venue in the frontmatter remains named for adjudication of any of the three conditions if they arise.

### Terminology and data-element definition is delegated to the Core Data Dictionary

An earlier draft of this entry contained extensive inline terminology binding guidance (SNOMED `71854001`, BodyStructure vs Observation resource selection, missing-data semantics, fallback strategies for systems without anatomical inventory). Per the April 2026 simulated review (Nash-Mendez and Leitner, converging from coordination and architecture angles), that work does not belong in a register entry. It belongs in the SMART Guidelines Core Data Dictionary, where data elements are defined once, bound once, and referenced by every DAK that needs them.

The inline guidance has been removed. The expected disposition is:

- A `HasCervix` Core Data Dictionary element should be proposed (if one does not already exist — the HIV DAK and the maternal health DAK may have an adjacent element that could be reused or extended) with a stable canonical URI.
- The Core Data Dictionary entry carries the terminology binding, the missing-data semantics, the FHIR profile guidance, and the jurisdictional fallback strategies.
- This register entry, once Core Data Dictionary integration is live, references the element by canonical URI in a single line and carries only the interpretive reasoning in its rationale.

Until the Core Data Dictionary path is integrated (see `../../../methodology/findings-from-simulated-review.md` Section 7 Blocker 3), L3 implementers working from this entry will need to make the terminology and data-model decisions locally. That is expected and unavoidable at the current project maturity; it is recorded as technical debt to be repaid when ecosystem integration proceeds.

### Open review questions for the named venue

1. **The core interpretive question.** Which of the three options (`sex-female`, `gender-woman`, `has-cervix`) is the right resolution for the target jurisdiction, taking into account (a) the jurisdiction's position on the broader population coverage, (b) the data layer's capacity to express the chosen option, (c) consistency with adjacent DAKs running in the same country (particularly HPV vaccination and HIV screening DAKs), and (d) the direction of travel of the next WHO guideline revision. This is the decision that unblocks setting `selected`.
2. **Upstream dependency on the next WHO guideline revision.** Almonte's observation is that the Rec 1 / Section 3.1 compromise is an evidence-gap question that WHO is actively working on, and that more representative future trials may let Rec 1 itself broaden. The review venue should consider whether the honest answer for this DAK cycle is to hold the provisional encoding until the next revision and then harmonise with whatever the revised guideline says, rather than committing the current DAK to one of the three options in advance of evidence that could resolve the question upstream.
3. **Ripple into Treatment and Follow-up.** Does the population-definition question have operational implications further down the cascade (Treatment Decision, Follow-up Decision), or is it purely an eligibility concern? The provisional assumption is purely eligibility. A clinical reviewer should confirm that the downstream decisions are indifferent to how eligibility was established.
4. **Supracervical hysterectomy and other anatomical edge cases.** If the Core Data Dictionary `HasCervix` path is pursued, how should supracervical hysterectomy resolve? (Supracervical preserves the cervix; total does not. Most systems do not distinguish them.) This is an implementation detail for the Core Data Dictionary entry rather than for this register entry, but it is flagged here so the Core Data Dictionary proposal does not miss it.
